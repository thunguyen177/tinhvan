“Fine. The possible events are

1111;0000

1110;1101;1011;0111 (1 female, 3 females)

1100;0011;1001;0101;1010;0110 (2 males 2 females)

0001;0010;0100;1000 (3 females, 1 male)

So, the probability of 3 males, 1 female or 3 females, 1 male is 4/16+4/16=1/2.

The probability of 2 females, 2 males, is 6/16=3/8.

So, the probability of 3 males, 1 female and the probability of 3 females, 1 male, both are bigger than the probability of 2 males, 2 females."

“Alright, in reality, there are a lot of random variables that have values are 0 or 1 like this. For example, one can win or lose a game, the moon may or may not appear during a night, etc.” Then, we can model these events using Bernoulli distribution.

To understand this, you need to understand statistical distributions. For a discrete random variable with its corresponding event space $\Omega$, we define $$p_X(x) =P(X=x)\forall x\in \Omega.$$ as the probability mass function of X. This measure the probability that X receive the value $x$.

If you notice carefully, this notation is just a lazy way to write $P(X=x)$ by removing the $=$ sign and put $X$ to the subscript.

Obviously, $$0\le p_{X}(x)\le 1 \;\forall x\in \Omega.$$ and Of course, the probabilities of all the events in the event space need to sum up to 1. Therefore $$\sum _{x\in \Omega}{p_{X}}(x)=1$$

Come back to the cat problem. The Bernoulli distribution denoted as $Ber(p)$ is a discrete distribution that only receives value 0 or 1. Here, $p$ is the probability that the variable gets value 1, i.e., $p = P(X=1)$. Usually, $p$ is considered as the probability of success when doing something. Then, of course, the probability of a failure, which usually referred to as the probability of X, receiving value 0, is $$\mathbf{P} (X=0)=1-\mathbf{P} (X=1)=1-p.$$

Yet, your cat will give birth to four kitties, not one. If we consider the gender of each kitty to be independent then we can use (binomial distribution) instead of the Bernoulli model. The binomial distribution, denoted as $Binom(n,p)$, can help us model the possibility of $k$ success out of $n$ trials.

I mean, if you toss a coins $n$ times, independently, and the probability of getting a head (success) in each toss is $p$, then the probability of getting $k$ heads (success) is

$$\mathbf{P} (X=k)={n \choose k}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}\quad {\mbox{where}}\ k=0,1,2,\dots ,n$$

and that's the distribution of a random variable $Binom(n,p)$."

"This sound like repeating the Bernoulli model $n$ times?" Parker asked.

"True. In fact, if we have $n$ independent random Bernoulli variable $X_1,...,X_n\sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ then $$\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\sim Binom(n,p)$$

How many trials for the questions I asked you?"

"4"

"Ok. Then, $n = 4$. What's the probability of success?"

"$p=\frac{1}{2}$ because the probability of male and female are equal in this example"

"Then, the probability of getting 2 males, 2 females can be computed directly by using probability distribution function:

$$P(X = 2) = {n\choose k}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k} = {4 \choose 2}\left(\frac{1}{2} \right )^{2}(1-\frac{1}{2})^{4-2} = \frac{3}{8}$$

The probability of 1 male, 3 females is

$$P(X = 1) = {n \choose k}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k} = {4 \choose 1}\left(\frac{1}{2} \right )^{1}(1-\frac{1}{2})^{4-1} = \frac{1}{4}$$

The probability of 3 male, 1 female, is

$$P(X = 3) = {n \choose k}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k} $$ $$= {4 \choose 3}\left(\frac{1}{2} \right )^{3}(1-\frac{1}{2})^{4-3} = \frac{1}{4}$$

"So, the probability of giving birth to at most 2 males is $$P(X\le 2)=P(X=0)+P(X=1)+P(X=2)=\frac{1}{16}+\frac{3}{8}+\frac{1}{4}?"$$

"Yes, but we can also compute this probability by using Cumulative distribution function,, which is commonly shorthanded as CDF . For each real number $x$, the Cumulative distribution function can be understood as the probability that a random variable is smaller or equal to x , i.e., $$F_X(x)=P(X\leq x)$$ The Cumulative distribution function of a binomial distribution is $$F_X(k)=P(X\leq k)=\sum _{i=0}^k{\binom {n}{i}p^{i}(1-p)^{n-i}}$$ where $k$ is the number of success out of $n$ trials.

"So, the probability of giving birth to at most two males is

$$F_X(2)=P(X\leq 2)=\sum _{i=0}^2{\binom {4}{i}}\left(\frac{1}{2} \right )^{i}\left(1-\frac{1}{2} \right )^{4-i}=\frac{5}{8}$$

"

"But what if I want to know the probability of giving birth to 2 or 3 males?" Parker asked.

Mai replied,

"$P(2\le X\le 3)= P(X\le 3)-P(X<2)$? Am I right? Because $\{2\le X\le 3\}$ and $\{X<2\}$ are non-overlapping, i.e.
$$P(2\le X\le 3)+P(X<2)=P(\{2\le X\le 3\}\cup\{X<2\})=P(X\le 3)!$$

So,

$$P(2\le X\le 3)= F_X(3)-F_X(1).$$

Yet, $$F_X(1)= P(X=0)+P(X=1)= \frac{1}{16}+\frac{3}{8}=\frac{7}{16}$$ $$F_X(3)=\sum _{i=0}^3{\binom {4}{i}}\left(\frac{1}{2} \right )^{i}\left(1-\frac{1}{2} \right )^{4-i}=\frac{15}{16}$$

So, the probability of giving birth to 2 or 3 males is

$$P(2\le X\le 3)= F_X(3)-F_X(1)=\frac{15}{16}-\frac{7}{16}=\frac{8}{16}=\frac{1}{2}.$$
