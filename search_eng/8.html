How to manipulate a poll

Sometimes, the way people read and write about statistics or learn from prediction failures can be pretty funny. Years ago, in the land of Ironic, it was known that people predicted by a survey that Mr. Mickey would win the presidential position. Yet, it turned out that Mr. Nemo won it instead. Later, they realized that the problem was that they did the survey via phone call, and the fishes, most of the time, were too busy swimming to pick up the phones. In addition, most of them dont even have a phone, unlike the other residents that live on land. So, the sample they collected by surveying through the phones did not reflect the whole population!

This is repeated in a slightly different manner in the land of Genome. A local newspaper, Lahey, surveyed its readers and concluded from the survey that Skeropt would win, but Lucenberg won the election instead. Well, this is understandable because the magazine is a local one. So, most of its readers are local. Therefore, instead of drawing out that conclusion, perhaps they should have concluded that the people in that region are more likely to vote for Skeropt.

Well, perhaps you should not laugh at them like I did. Its tough to get a sample that reflects the whole population! If you were to catch fishes in a pond to study using a net, then if the net holes are too big, you cant get the small fishes. So, your sample does not reflect the population.  If you were to fish them instead, then you may not be able to get the strong, big one that can get out of the hook when you try to pool them up.

But nothings wrong with that for shadowy people who like to manipulate the data to their advantage.

Vote for Lucky!

Another presidential election again! I'm the advisor of candidate Lucky. Lucky for him, I'm a statistician, and I know how to manipulate people using data. Also, we learned a lot from past elections. During the last election, candidate Hilton was widely believed to be elected. The survey unit provided compelling statistical results from the polls and minute details on their reasonable sampling strategy. Since it was widely believed that she would win anyway, a lot of people didn't submit their votes for her due to bad weather or since the voting submission is too complicated Those people probably thought that missing one vote wouldn't affect the election result. But it was a pity because many other people also thought so! On the other hand, after hearing about the poll, the supporters of Hilton's competitor (Mr. Spiderman) tried to get everyone they could get to vote for him. It finally came out that Mr. Spiderman won the election.

So, I have a strategy. I will create a poll and manipulate it so that the outcome is that Mr. Lucky will be lost to his competitor, Mr. Shadow. Then, the supporters of Mr. Shadow can feel free to be lazy and stay home instead of going to voting sites to vote for him. Meanwhile, the supporters of Mr. Lucky will try their best to get more votes for him.

Not that I faked the data or anything. I made a poll and reported how it was conducted in a truthful manner to the newspaper because if the information of faking data leaks out, it could create consequences for my candidate. The thing is, many people, upon reading the news, account for it as facts rather than an estimated probability. However, statistics can be very easily manipulated. Different sampling methods (simple random sampling/systematic sampling/ stratified sampling/) may lead to different conclusions about the population. Its not something wrong with the methods, but it just depends on the nature of the problem that the statistician should choose a suitable one in order to get a sample that represents the population.

That is one aspect. I will not go too deep into politics (I mean, I will not tell you how we played the tricks, ha ha ha). However, for the survey, the way the questions are phrased can lead to different answers from the participants. Also, open questions may take too much time to think about, and people may skip that or fill in random thoughts. Not only that, even for multiple-choice questions, the order of the options may affect the responses as well, as if love at first sight!! Yep, thats true, and one way to improve this issue is to randomize the order of the options. Also, the previously asked questions in the survey may affect the responses of the next ones. . So, to reduce the bias in a survey, one good strategy is to have a small group of people fill out the survey and evaluate its design and clarity before officially conducting the survey. In our case, to manipulate the poll, we actually did the reverse. We had a team evaluate the survey and gather ideas to increase the bias!!!
